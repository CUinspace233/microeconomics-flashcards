# Chapter 8 Exam Review Outline — Oligopoly and Game Theory

## Core ideas
- Oligopoly is a market with a small number of firms.
- Because there are only a few firms, each firm’s choice affects rivals and each firm must think strategically.
- Game theory is used to analyze these strategic interactions.
- Oligopoly often creates outcomes where individually rational behaviour is not collectively best.

## Key definitions
- **Oligopoly**: market structure with a small number of firms.
- **Strategic interaction**: one firm’s payoff depends on actions chosen by other firms.
- **Game theory**: study of how decision-makers behave when outcomes depend on interaction with others.
- **Business strategy**: a set of actions taken to achieve a goal such as profit maximization.
- **Simultaneous game**: players move at the same time or without knowing the other player’s move.
- **Payoff matrix**: table showing payoffs for every combination of strategies.

## Dominant strategy and Nash equilibrium
- **Dominant strategy**: a strategy preferred regardless of what the other player does.
- **Nash equilibrium**: a strategy profile in which no player wants to change strategy given the strategy chosen by the other player.
- A Nash equilibrium need not be the best joint outcome.

## Prisoner’s dilemma
- A prisoner’s dilemma occurs when each player follows self-interest / a dominant strategy and both end up worse off than under cooperation.
- This shows that the invisible hand can fail in oligopoly settings.
- In price competition, firms may both cut price even though both would earn higher profits if both kept price high.

## Entry game intuition
- In oligopoly and entry problems, firms must anticipate how rivals will respond.
- Rationality means each player assumes the other also acts to maximize its own payoff.

## Cartels
- **Cartel**: private agreement among firms to reduce competition and raise joint profits.
- Cartels try to mimic monopoly behaviour.
- Cartels are generally illegal under competition law.
- Main weakness: members have an incentive to cheat by secretly cutting price or expanding output.

## Coordination games
- **Coordination game**: game in which players benefit from coordinating their choices.
- Such games can have multiple Nash equilibria.
- Example idea: Battle of the Sexes — both want to coordinate, but disagree on which coordinated outcome is best.

## Exam traps
- A dominant strategy is about one player’s best action regardless of others; a Nash equilibrium is about mutual best responses.
- A Nash equilibrium is not necessarily Pareto optimal.
- In a prisoner’s dilemma, cooperation is collectively better but unstable.
- Cartel outcomes may be jointly profitable but individual firms have incentives to deviate.
