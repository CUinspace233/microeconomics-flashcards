# Chapter 10 Exam Review Outline — Public Goods

## Core ideas
- Public goods are defined by non-rivalry and non-excludability.
- Private markets usually under-provide public goods because of free-riding.
- For public goods, aggregate demand is found by vertical summation of individual marginal benefit curves.
- The efficient quantity satisfies the Samuelson condition.
- Government provision financed by taxation is the standard remedy, though it is imperfect because people have incentives to understate valuations.

## Key definitions
- **Non-rivalry**: one person’s consumption does not reduce another person’s ability to consume the good; marginal cost of an extra user is zero.
- **Non-excludability**: people cannot easily be prevented from consuming the good.
- **Pure public good**: perfectly non-rival and non-excludable.
- **Impure public good**: non-rival and/or non-excludable only up to a point.

## Examples
- National defense, judiciary system, some health and education services in lecture framing.
- Pure vs impure distinctions matter when congestion or excludability becomes relevant.

## Public goods vs private goods
- For a private good, market demand is the horizontal sum of individual demands.
- For a public good, marginal social benefit is the vertical sum of individual marginal benefits.
- Reason: all consumers enjoy the same quantity of the public good.

## Marginal social benefit and efficiency
- **Marginal social benefit (MSB)** = vertical sum of individual marginal benefits.
- **Samuelson condition**: efficient quantity is where the sum of individual marginal benefits equals marginal cost.
  - <code>ΣMB = MC</code>

## Free-riding
- **Free-riding**: enjoying a public good without paying for it.
- Because the good is non-excludable, each person hopes others will pay.
- Result: market under-provision of public goods.

## Lindahl prices
- **Lindahl prices**: each person pays according to their marginal benefit from the public good.
- In theory, this can support the efficient quantity.
- In practice, it fails because people have incentives to understate their true valuation and free-ride.

## Worked cleaner example from lecture
- Anna’s demand: <code>P = 50 - 5Q</code>
- Zoe’s demand: <code>P = 25 - 2.5Q</code>
- Marginal social benefit: <code>P = 75 - 7.5Q</code>
- If marginal cost is <code>30</code>:
  - <code>30 = 75 - 7.5Q</code>
  - <code>Q = 6</code>
- Lindahl prices at <code>Q = 6</code>:
  - Anna pays <code>50 - 5(6) = 20</code>
  - Zoe pays <code>25 - 2.5(6) = 10</code>
  - total = <code>30</code> which covers MC
- But private market provision under-provides because each person has an incentive to free-ride.

## Public goods and externalities
- A public good is an extreme case of positive externality.
- The benefit to others does not depend on who personally paid for the provision.
- This is linked to non-rivalry and non-excludability.

## Government provision
- Standard remedy: government taxes people and provides the public good.
- Problem: individual valuations are private information, so it is hard to know the true efficient quantity and fair tax shares.
- Lecture fairness ideas:
  - ability-to-pay principle
  - pay-as-you-go principle

## Exam traps
- Do not use horizontal summation for public-good demand; use vertical summation.
- Public goods are under-provided, not over-provided, by private markets.
- Lindahl pricing is a theoretical benchmark, not a realistic fully reliable solution.
- Non-rivalry and non-excludability are separate concepts; a good can be impure if only one or neither holds fully.
