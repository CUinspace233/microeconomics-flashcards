# Chapter 9 Exam Review Outline — Externalities

## Core ideas
- An externality is a cost or benefit that falls on someone not directly involved in the market transaction.
- When externalities exist, private decisions do not generally maximize social surplus.
- Positive externalities lead to underproduction or underconsumption from society’s point of view.
- Negative externalities lead to overproduction or overconsumption from society’s point of view.
- If bargaining is possible and transaction costs are zero, Coase theorem says private bargaining can reach efficiency.
- In large markets, bargaining is usually impractical, so taxes or subsidies may be needed.

## Classification
- Positive consumption externality
- Negative consumption externality
- Positive production externality
- Negative production externality

## Positive consumption externality
- Someone else benefits when a consumer consumes the good.
- Social marginal benefit exceeds private marginal benefit:
  - <code>MSB = MPB + MEB</code>
- Private choice under-consumes relative to the social optimum.
- Efficient response in large markets: subsidy equal to the marginal external benefit.
- Examples: education, vaccinations, fitness, bike-to-work.

## Negative production externality
- Someone else bears a cost when the firm produces the good.
- Social marginal cost exceeds private marginal cost:
  - <code>MSC = MPC + MEC</code>
- Private market over-produces relative to the social optimum.
- Efficient response in large markets: tax equal to the marginal external cost.
- Examples: pollution, over-fishing, harmful production activities.

## Negative consumption externality
- Someone else bears a cost when a consumer consumes the good.
- Social marginal benefit is below private marginal benefit.
- Private market over-consumes relative to the social optimum.
- Examples: smoking, alcohol abuse, dangerous driving.

## Positive production externality
- Someone else benefits when a firm produces the good.
- Social marginal cost is below private marginal cost, or equivalently social benefit exceeds private benefit depending on framing.
- Private market under-produces relative to the social optimum.
- Examples: innovation spillovers, beneficial production technologies, on-the-job training.

## Coase theorem
- If trade in the externality is possible and there are no transaction costs, bargaining can achieve the efficient outcome regardless of the initial allocation of property rights.
- This works best when only a small number of parties are involved and negotiation is feasible.
- It tends to fail in large markets with many affected parties because transaction costs are high.

## Pigovian policy
- Positive externality -> subsidy equal to marginal external benefit.
- Negative externality -> tax equal to marginal external cost.
- Goal: align private incentives with social costs and benefits.

## Worked lecture-style example: tax for a negative production externality
- Demand: <code>Pd = 40 - 0.2Q</code>
- Private supply: <code>Ps = 0.2Q</code>
- Private equilibrium:
  - <code>40 - 0.2Q = 0.2Q</code>
  - <code>Q = 100</code>, price <code>= 20</code>
- Suppose marginal external cost is 2, so optimal tax is 2.
- New supply: <code>Ps = 0.2Q + 2</code>
- New equilibrium:
  - <code>40 - 0.2Q = 0.2Q + 2</code>
  - <code>Q = 95</code>
  - buyer price <code>= 21</code>
  - seller net price <code>= 19</code>
- Government revenue: <code>2 × 95 = 190</code>
- Lecture numbers:
  - CS = <code>902.5</code>
  - PS = <code>902.5</code>
  - Government revenue = <code>190</code>
  - Total surplus = <code>1995</code>
  - DWL = <code>5</code>

## Exam traps
- Always distinguish private from social cost/benefit curves.
- Positive externality usually means underproduction/underconsumption; negative externality usually means overproduction/overconsumption.
- Coase theorem requires low or zero transaction costs and feasible bargaining.
- Pigovian tax/subsidy should match the external cost/benefit at the efficient quantity.
