# Chapter 5 Exam Review Outline — Government Intervention: The Cost of Interfering with Market Forces

## Core ideas
- Competitive markets tend to move toward equilibrium.
- Policies that prevent the market from reaching equilibrium usually reduce total surplus.
- Main interventions in this chapter:
  - price ceilings
  - price floors
  - taxes
  - subsidies
- Core exam language: binding vs non-binding, shortage vs surplus, tax wedge, incidence, government revenue / spending, deadweight loss.

## Price ceiling
- A price ceiling is a maximum legal price.
- It is binding only if set below equilibrium price.
- If set above equilibrium price, it has no effect.
- A binding price ceiling causes:
  - lower market price
  - lower quantity traded
  - excess demand / shortage
- Not all consumers gain; only those who still manage to buy at the lower price gain.
- Allocation may depend on rationing, queues, favoritism, or non-price methods.

## Price floor
- A price floor is a minimum legal price.
- It is binding only if set above equilibrium price.
- If set below equilibrium price, it has no effect.
- A binding price floor causes:
  - higher market price
  - lower quantity traded
  - excess supply / surplus
- Some producers may gain, but buyers lose and some sellers may be unable to sell.

## Deadweight loss
- Deadweight loss is the loss of total surplus caused by output being prevented from reaching the efficient equilibrium quantity.
- In competitive markets, the efficient point is where MB = MC.
- Price ceilings, price floors, taxes, and subsidies can all create deadweight loss.

## Taxation
- A per-unit tax creates a wedge between the price paid by buyers and the price received by sellers.
- If imposed on producers, supply shifts upward by the amount of the tax.
- If imposed on buyers, demand shifts downward by the amount of the tax.
- Equilibrium quantity falls after a tax.
- The buyer price rises, the seller net price falls, and the gap equals the tax.

## Tax incidence
- The side of the market that is less elastic bears more of the tax burden.
- If demand is very inelastic, consumers bear more of the tax.
- If supply is very inelastic, producers bear more of the tax.
- Legal incidence and economic incidence are not the same thing.

## Tax revenue and deadweight loss
- Government revenue = tax per unit × quantity sold after tax.
- Deadweight loss from a tax is the triangle from lost trades.
- More elastic demand and supply create a larger deadweight loss for a given tax.
- If the government must raise revenue, taxing less elastic markets causes less distortion.

## Subsidy
- A subsidy is the opposite of a tax.
- If paid to producers, supply shifts downward by the subsidy amount.
- Consumer price falls, producer effective price rises, and quantity increases.
- Government spending = subsidy per unit × quantity after subsidy.
- Subsidies can create deadweight loss because they push output above the efficient level.

## Policy evaluation themes
- Efficiency and fairness are different questions.
- A policy can help some people yet still reduce total surplus.
- If the policy goal is redistribution, a direct transfer may be more efficient than distorting market prices.

## Worked examples from lecture style
### Example 1: Price ceiling
- Demand: Qd = 36 - 2P
- Supply: Qs = P
- Equilibrium:
  - 36 - 2P = P
  - P* = 12, Q* = 12
- If a binding ceiling sets P = 8:
  - Qd = 20
  - Qs = 8
  - quantity traded = 8
  - shortage = 12

### Example 2: Tax on producers
- Demand: P = 60 - 0.2Q
- Supply: P = 0.2Q
- Before tax:
  - 60 - 0.2Q = 0.2Q
  - Q* = 150, P* = 30
- After a $10 tax on producers:
  - new supply: P = 0.2Q + 10
  - 60 - 0.2Q = 0.2Q + 10
  - Q = 125
  - buyer price = 35
  - seller net price = 25
  - tax revenue = 10 × 125 = 1250
  - DWL = 125

## Exam traps
- Always test whether the ceiling or floor is binding.
- Under price controls, quantity traded is limited by the short side of the market.
- For a tax, distinguish buyer price from seller price.
- Do not assume statutory incidence determines real burden.
- When calculating deadweight loss, use the reduction in quantity relative to equilibrium.
