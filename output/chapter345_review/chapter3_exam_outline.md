# Chapter 3 Exam Review Outline — Demand in a Perfectly Competitive Market

## Core ideas
- Demand comes from consumer choice under the cost-benefit principle.
- For a consumer, keep consuming extra units while marginal benefit is at least marginal cost.
- In a perfectly competitive market, the marginal cost of one more unit consumed is the market price.
- Quantity demanded is the quantity that maximizes utility at the given price.
- The demand curve shows the relationship between price and quantity demanded.
- In the continuous model, the demand curve is the consumer’s marginal benefit curve.

## Utility and marginal utility
- Utility: satisfaction from consuming goods or taking actions.
- Marginal utility: extra utility from one more unit.
- Decreasing marginal utility: extra satisfaction from additional units tends to fall as more units are consumed.
- Because marginal utility falls, a lower price is required to justify consuming extra units.

## Law of demand
- Law of demand: as price falls, quantity demanded tends to rise, other things equal.
- Two main reasons:
  - **Substitution effect**: when a good becomes cheaper relative to other goods, consumers substitute toward it.
  - **Income effect**: a lower price raises real purchasing power.

## Income effect by type of good
- **Normal good**: higher income increases quantity demanded.
- **Inferior good**: higher income decreases quantity demanded.
- Usually substitution and income effects work in the same direction for normal goods.
- For inferior goods, the income effect opposes the substitution effect.
- **Giffen good**: a rare inferior good where the negative income effect is so strong that it outweighs the substitution effect, so a price fall can reduce quantity demanded.
- Exam note: a Giffen good is not the same as a Veblen good.

## Reservation price and willingness to pay
- A point on the demand curve can be read in two directions:
  - given price -> quantity demanded
  - given quantity -> the price on the curve is the maximum willingness to pay for the marginal unit
- This price is the consumer reservation price for that marginal unit.

## Movements vs shifts
- A change in the good’s own price causes a movement along the demand curve.
- A change in other determinants causes the demand curve to shift.

## Demand shifters
- Preferences / successful advertising -> demand shifts right
- Price of a substitute rises -> demand for this good shifts right
- Price of a complement rises -> demand for this good shifts left
- Income rises:
  - normal good -> demand shifts right
  - inferior good -> demand shifts left
- Expected future price rises -> current demand shifts right
- Population / number of buyers rises -> market demand shifts right

## Substitutes and complements
- **Substitutes**: if the price of one good rises, demand for the other rises.
- **Complements**: if the price of one good rises, demand for the other falls.

## Price elasticity of demand
- Measures responsiveness of quantity demanded to price.
- Formula:
  - PED = (% change in Qd) / (% change in P)
- Because demand slopes downward, the signed elasticity is negative.
- By convention, elasticity is usually reported in absolute value.
- Point elasticity on a linear demand curve:
  - PED = (1 / slope) × (P / Q)

## Elasticity terminology
- Elastic demand: PED > 1
- Unit elastic demand: PED = 1
- Inelastic demand: PED < 1

## What affects demand elasticity
- Availability of close substitutes: more substitutes -> more elastic
- Narrow vs broad definition of the good: narrowly defined goods -> more elastic
- Share of income: larger budget share -> more elastic
- Time horizon: demand is more elastic in the long run

## Elasticity along a linear demand curve
- Upper part: elastic
- Midpoint: unit elastic
- Lower part: inelastic

## Revenue and elasticity
- Total revenue = P × Q
- If demand is elastic, a price cut increases total revenue.
- If demand is inelastic, a price cut decreases total revenue.
- If demand is unit elastic, a price cut leaves total revenue unchanged.

## Exam traps
- Do not confuse a movement along demand with a shift of demand.
- Do not forget that the signed price elasticity of demand is negative, but many answers use the absolute value.
- A Giffen good is rare and requires the income effect to dominate the substitution effect.
- Reservation price is the maximum willingness to pay of the marginal buyer, not necessarily average willingness to pay.
