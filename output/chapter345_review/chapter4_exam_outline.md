# Chapter 4 Exam Review Outline — Demand & Supply: Equilibrium Analysis

## Core ideas
- Market demand and market supply come from horizontal aggregation of individual demand and supply curves.
- Market equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.
- In a perfectly competitive market, buyers and sellers are price takers.
- Equilibrium maximizes total surplus in the competitive benchmark.

## Aggregation
- Aggregate demand = horizontal sum of individual demand curves.
- Aggregate supply = horizontal sum of individual supply curves.
- Horizontal sum means add quantities at each price.
- Exam rule: do not vertically add prices.

## Equilibrium
- Equilibrium price and quantity satisfy:
  - Qd = Qs
- To solve algebraically:
  1. write demand and supply equations
  2. set quantity demanded equal to quantity supplied
  3. solve for equilibrium price or quantity
  4. substitute back to get the other variable
- At equilibrium there is no pressure for price to change.

## Excess demand and excess supply
- **Excess demand**: quantity demanded > quantity supplied -> shortage
- **Excess supply**: quantity supplied > quantity demanded -> surplus
- If price is below equilibrium, shortage puts upward pressure on price.
- If price is above equilibrium, surplus puts downward pressure on price.

## Reservation prices
- Buyer reservation price: highest price a buyer is willing to pay.
- Seller reservation price: lowest price a seller is willing to accept.
- For buyers, reservation price reflects marginal benefit / willingness to pay.
- For sellers, reservation price reflects marginal cost.

## Rationing rule
- Buyers who value the good more are the first to buy it.
- This means units go to buyers with the highest reservation prices first.

## Surplus
- Consumer surplus = willingness to pay - price actually paid.
- Producer surplus = price received - seller reservation price.
- Total consumer surplus = sum across buyers.
- Total producer surplus = sum across sellers.
- Total surplus = consumer surplus + producer surplus.

## Why equilibrium is efficient
- In perfect competition, equilibrium output is where MB = MC.
- Total surplus is maximized at equilibrium.
- If output is below equilibrium, there are unrealized gains from trade.
- If output is above equilibrium, some units cost more to produce than consumers value them.

## Pareto efficiency
- Pareto efficiency: no one can be made better off without making someone else worse off.
- A Pareto-improving transaction makes all involved parties better off.
- Competitive equilibrium is Pareto efficient in the standard benchmark because all mutually beneficial trades have been exhausted.

## Invisible hand principle
- Individuals pursuing private gains can lead to a socially efficient allocation of resources in competitive markets.
- Buyers maximize utility; sellers maximize profit.
- Under competition and no major distortions, this process pushes the market toward equilibrium.

## Short run vs long run market adjustment
- In the short run, firms respond with output changes along supply curves.
- If firms earn positive economic profit, entry occurs in the long run.
- Entry shifts market supply right and pushes price down.
- If firms incur losses, exit occurs in the long run.
- Exit shifts market supply left and pushes price up.
- Long-run competitive equilibrium has:
  - zero economic profit
  - price equal to minimum ATC
  - more elastic market supply than in the short run

## Exam traps
- Aggregate curves are horizontal sums, not vertical sums.
- Equilibrium is about Qd = Qs, not MB = price alone or MC = price alone in isolation.
- Reservation prices differ across units; think marginal unit, not average valuation.
- Pareto efficiency does not mean equality or fairness.
- Equity and efficiency are different concepts.
