# Chapter 7 Exam Review Outline — Market Power and Monopoly

## Core ideas
- Imperfect competition arises when one or more conditions of perfect competition fail.
- A firm with market power can influence price and is therefore a price maker.
- Monopoly is the extreme case: one firm serves the whole market.
- A monopolist chooses output where marginal revenue equals marginal cost, then uses the demand curve to set price.
- Monopoly leads to allocative inefficiency because price exceeds marginal cost and output is below the socially efficient quantity.

## Imperfect vs perfect competition
Perfect competition assumes:
- price-taking buyers and sellers
- homogeneous goods
- no externalities
- excludable and rival goods
- full information
- free entry and exit

Imperfect competition arises when one or more of these assumptions fail.

## Market power
- **Price maker / price setter**: a firm that can set its own price.
- **Market power**: the ability to set price rather than taking it as given.
- Types mentioned in lecture:
  - monopoly: one firm
  - monopolistic competition: many firms selling differentiated products
  - oligopoly: a small number of firms selling close substitutes

## Free entry and barriers to entry
- Free entry and exit is the main antidote to market power.
- Barriers to entry protect incumbents and allow market power to persist.

## Increasing returns to scale and natural monopoly
- **Increasing returns to scale / economies of scale**: average cost falls as output rises.
- This can make one large firm more efficient than many small firms.
- **Natural monopoly**: a monopoly that exists because increasing returns to scale make a single-firm structure most efficient.

## Monopoly basics
- Monopoly: a market structure with a single seller.
- Because the monopolist faces the market demand curve, its own demand curve is the market demand curve.
- To sell more, the monopolist must lower price.

## Revenue and marginal revenue
- Total revenue = <code>P × Q</code>.
- For a monopolist, marginal revenue is below price because lowering price to sell one more unit reduces revenue on all units sold.
- This is why the MR curve lies below the demand curve.

## Profit maximization under monopoly
1. Find the quantity where <code>MR = MC</code>.
2. Move up to the demand curve to find the monopoly price.
3. Profit = <code>(P - ATC) × Q</code> if price is above ATC.

## Monopoly vs social optimum
- Socially efficient output occurs where <code>P = MC</code> (equivalently, MB = MC).
- Monopoly output satisfies <code>MR = MC</code>, so:
  - <code>Qm < Qefficient</code>
  - <code>Pm > MC</code>
- Result: allocative inefficiency and deadweight loss.

## Government regulation
### Competition law
- Competition law aims to limit anti-competitive conduct and promote competition.

### Marginal cost pricing
- Force the monopolist to charge price equal to marginal cost.
- This achieves allocative efficiency.
- But for natural monopolies, if price is below average cost, the firm may make losses and the industry may collapse unless subsidized.

### Average cost pricing
- Force the monopolist to set price where demand intersects ATC.
- This gives zero economic profit (normal profit).
- It avoids collapse but still leaves allocative inefficiency because price remains above marginal cost.
- It is also hard to implement and can weaken incentives to cut costs.

## Price discrimination
### First-degree price discrimination
- The monopolist charges each consumer exactly their reservation price.
- Output expands to the socially efficient quantity.
- Consumer surplus is eliminated; the monopolist captures the surplus.

### Second-degree price discrimination
- Different prices based on the quantity purchased or version selected.
- Example: bulk discounts.

### Third-degree price discrimination
- Different prices for different consumer groups or markets.
- Example: student discounts or pricing by location.

## Exam traps
- A monopolist does not choose price where demand equals MC; it chooses quantity where MR = MC and then reads price from demand.
- Demand is not the same as MR under monopoly.
- Allocative efficiency requires <code>P = MC</code>, not <code>MR = MC</code>.
- Average cost pricing gives zero economic profit, not allocative efficiency.
- First-degree price discrimination can remove deadweight loss but transfers surplus from consumers to the firm.
